📊 How to Interpret Adverse Event Data

The FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) is a voluntary reporting database. Healthcare providers, patients, and manufacturers submit reports of suspected adverse drug reactions.

  • Correlation ≠ Causation: A reported reaction doesn't prove the drug caused it — the patient may have had other conditions or medications.
  • More reports ≠ more dangerous: Popular, widely-prescribed drugs naturally accumulate more reports simply because more people take them.
  • Underreporting is common: Estimates suggest only 1–10% of adverse events are actually reported to FAERS.
  • Use patterns, not absolutes: This data is best used to identify signals and patterns for further research, not to draw definitive conclusions.

Always consult a healthcare professional before changing any medication regimen.

🔔 Understanding Recall Classifications

The FDA classifies drug recalls into three categories based on potential health risk:

  • Class I: The most serious type. Use of or exposure to the product will likely cause serious adverse health consequences or death. Example: contaminated injectable medication.
  • Class II: Use may cause temporary or medically reversible adverse health consequences. Example: incorrect potency — drug is less effective than stated.
  • Class III: Use or exposure is unlikely to cause any adverse health consequence. Example: labeling error with no safety impact.

A drug having past recalls doesn't necessarily mean it is currently unsafe — recalls are often precautionary.

⚠ Drug Pairs with Known Dangerous Interactions

  • Warfarin + NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen, etc.): NSAIDs inhibit platelet function and may damage the GI lining. Combined with warfarin's anticoagulant effect, this dramatically increases bleeding risk.
  • MAO Inhibitors + SSRIs: Together these can cause serotonin syndrome — a potentially fatal condition with symptoms including agitation, rapid heart rate, high blood pressure, and hyperthermia.
  • Methotrexate + NSAIDs: NSAIDs reduce renal clearance of methotrexate, causing toxic buildup. Can lead to severe bone marrow suppression.

This list is illustrative, not exhaustive. Always consult a pharmacist or physician before combining medications.

🏷 What Drug Labels Actually Tell You

FDA drug labels (also called "prescribing information" or "package inserts") are the most authoritative source of drug safety information.

  • Black-Box Warnings: The most serious FDA warning. Required when there is a significant risk of serious or life-threatening adverse reactions. Printed inside a black box at the top of the label.
  • Indications: FDA-approved uses for the drug. "Off-label" use (not listed) is legal but occurs at the physician's discretion.
  • Contraindications: Situations where the drug should NOT be used under any circumstances.
  • Interactions: Known interactions with other drugs, foods, or conditions.

Labels are reviewed by the FDA before drug approval and updated as new safety information emerges.

📈 Why Some Drugs Have More Reports Than Others

The number of FAERS adverse event reports for a drug does not directly reflect how dangerous it is. Several factors influence report volume:

  • Prescription volume: A drug taken by millions will have more reports purely because of exposure — even if its risk per person is low.
  • Drug age: Older drugs have decades of accumulated reports. Newer drugs have fewer reports simply because they've been around less time.
  • Media coverage: High-profile safety alerts or news stories dramatically increase reporting for specific drugs.
  • Mandatory reporting: Manufacturers must report certain serious events; patient and provider reporting is voluntary and underrepresents true incidence.
  • Drug class awareness: Drugs in classes known for adverse effects (e.g., blood thinners) tend to have higher reporting rates.

ℹ About Drug Safety Explorer

Drug Safety Explorer is an educational tool built for AIML 1870 at the University of Nebraska at Omaha.

Data sources: All data is retrieved in real-time from the OpenFDA API (open.fda.gov). No API key is required. Data includes FDA drug labels, FAERS adverse event reports, and FDA enforcement (recall) records.

Limitations:

  • FAERS data is voluntary and subject to reporting bias.
  • Absence of data does not imply safety.
  • This tool does not provide medical advice.
  • Drug name matching may not capture all formulations.
